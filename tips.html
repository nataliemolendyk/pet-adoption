Pet Care Tips

Helpful advice for new and prospective pet owners to ensure a happy, healthy life for your new companion.

Pet Care Tips

Expert advice compiled from the ASPCA, AVMA, and Humane Society.

Preparing Your Home for a New Pet

Before bringing your new pet home, the ASPCA recommends taking time to prepare your space:[1]

  • Pet-proof your home: Secure loose wires, remove toxic plants (check the ASPCA's toxic plant list), and store chemicals out of reach.
  • Create a safe zone: Set up a quiet area with a bed, water bowl, and toys where your pet can retreat. Consider using gates to limit access to specific areas.
  • Stock up on essentials: Food and water bowls, collar and leash, ID tags, bed, crate (if using), toys, and grooming supplies. For cats, include a litter box, litter, and scratching posts.
  • Choose the right food: Ask the shelter what food your pet has been eating and stick with it initially to avoid stomach upset. Look for AAFCO-approved pet foods.
  • Schedule a vet visit: Book a checkup within the first week to establish care and discuss vaccinations.

Source: ASPCA — Adoption Tips

Your First Week with a New Pet

The first week is about building trust and establishing routines. The ASPCA's 3-3-3 Guideline explains what to expect:[1]

  • Go slow: Give your pet space to explore at their own pace. Don't overwhelm them with too many visitors. Your pet might feel nervous and overwhelmed encountering new sounds, smells, and people.
  • Establish a routine: Consistent feeding times, walk schedules, and bedtime help pets feel secure. Get into a routine as soon as possible.
  • Start training gently: Begin with basic commands like "sit" and "stay" using positive reinforcement. Stay calm and give clear direction.
  • Monitor eating and drinking: Some pets may be nervous eaters at first. Contact your vet if they refuse food for more than 24 hours.
  • Be patient: It can take about 3 weeks for a pet to start settling in and 3 months to fully adjust. Expect some accidents or anxious behavior — this is normal.

Source: ASPCA — Adoption Tips (3-3-3 Guideline)

Pet Nutrition Basics

Proper nutrition is key to your pet's health and longevity. The ASPCA offers these guidelines:[1]

  • Quality food matters: Look for AAFCO-approved pet foods formulated for your pet's life stage. The ASPCA notes that a good quality commercial pet food is often more cost-effective than a homemade diet.
  • Age-appropriate diet: Puppies/kittens, adults, and seniors have different nutritional needs. Choose food formulated for your pet's specific life stage.
  • Portion control: Follow feeding guidelines on the package and avoid free-feeding to prevent obesity. According to the AVMA, maintaining a healthy weight is one of the most important things you can do for your pet's overall health.[3]
  • Fresh water always: Ensure clean, fresh water is available at all times. Change it daily.
  • Avoid toxic foods: Never feed pets chocolate, grapes, raisins, onions, garlic, or xylitol (artificial sweetener). The ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center provides a complete list of toxic foods.[4]

Sources: ASPCA — Cutting Pet Care Costs | AVMA — Pet Care | ASPCA Poison Control

Training Tips

Training strengthens your bond and ensures good behavior. The ASPCA provides these professional recommendations:[5]

  • Use positive reinforcement: Reward good behavior with treats, praise, and play. Never use punishment — it can increase fear and anxiety.
  • Keep sessions short: 5-10 minute training sessions are more effective than long ones. Short, frequent sessions help maintain your pet's attention.
  • Be consistent: Use the same commands and rules from every family member. Consistency helps your pet understand what's expected.
  • Socialize early: Expose your pet to different people, animals, and environments in a controlled, positive way. The ASPCA recommends group classes for young puppies who need socialization.
  • Consider professional help: For serious behavior problems, the ASPCA recommends seeking a Certified Applied Animal Behaviorist (CAAB) or a veterinary behaviorist (Dip ACVB). For basic training, a Certified Professional Dog Trainer (CPDT) is a great option.

Source: ASPCA — Behavioral Help for Your Pet

Vet Care & Vaccinations

Regular veterinary care keeps your pet healthy. Recommendations from the ASPCA and AVMA:[3][6]

  • Annual checkups: Even healthy pets need yearly exams. The ASPCA warns that skipping regular check-ups can lead to more expensive and risky treatments down the road.
  • Core vaccinations: Dogs need rabies, distemper, parvovirus, and adenovirus (hepatitis). Cats need rabies, feline panleukopenia (distemper), feline calicivirus, and feline herpesvirus type I. Non-core vaccines are given based on lifestyle and exposure risk.[6]
  • Parasite prevention: Year-round flea, tick, and heartworm prevention is essential. The ASPCA notes that flea and tick infestations can cause costly medical problems from minor skin irritations to life-threatening blood loss.
  • Dental care: Brush your pet's teeth regularly and schedule professional cleanings as recommended. Dental disease can lead to heart and kidney problems if left untreated.
  • Spay/neuter: This prevents serious health problems including uterine, ovarian, and testicular cancers. Many local shelters provide resources for low- or no-cost spay/neuter surgeries.[1]

Sources: AVMA — Pet Care Resources | ASPCA — Vaccinations | ASPCA — Cutting Pet Care Costs
